# To Thine Own Self Be True — Ethics, Culture, and Bias

Reference for the role of ethics, organizational culture, cognitive biases, and self-awareness in strategic decision-making.

## The Problem of Self-Deception in Strategy

Maps reveal uncomfortable truths. The most important are about the organization itself:
- We are not as innovative as we claim
- We are building commodity components we should be buying
- Our purpose statement contradicts our actual investment priorities
- Our culture is misaligned with our stated strategy

> "To thine own self be true" — the map is honest even when the organization is not.

## Cognitive Biases in Strategic Decision-Making

Wardley identifies how human cognitive biases systematically distort strategic thinking:

### The Dunning-Kruger Problem in Strategy
People with low situational awareness are most confident in their strategic opinions. Maps challenge this:
- People who have never mapped a landscape think their intuition is sufficient
- Once you start mapping, you realize how much complexity you've been ignoring
- This creates resistance: "we don't need a map — I know this market"

**Fix**: Create a shared map and let the evidence challenge the opinions.

### Survivorship Bias
We study successful companies and copy their practices without knowing:
- How many companies with the same practices failed?
- Were the successful practices the cause of success, or coincidental?
- Does the successful company's landscape resemble ours?

**Map application**: Before copying a practice, map the landscape of the company you're copying. Does their situation resemble yours?

### Confirmation Bias in Map Building
When creating maps, teams tend to:
- Place components in stages that justify existing decisions
- Underestimate how far along the evolution axis a competitor has moved
- Overestimate their own components' uniqueness

**Fix**: External challenge sessions; ask someone with no stake in the outcome to critique the map.

### The Availability Heuristic
Recent, dramatic events are overweighted:
- "AI is transforming everything" (recent, dramatic)
- "We need to rebuild our entire product for AI" (reactive, possibly wrong)
- The map grounds this: where is AI on the evolution axis? What specific components are affected?

**Fix**: Maps require specific positions, not general narratives.

### The Narrative Fallacy
Humans create stories to explain patterns. These stories feel true but often aren't:
- "We succeed because we are customer-focused" (but many customer-focused companies fail)
- "We failed because of the market" (but competitors succeeded in the same market)

Maps replace narrative with positional analysis. The question is not "why did we succeed?" but "what was our position in the landscape, and was our strategy appropriate to that position?"

## Organizational Culture and the Map

### Culture as a Component
Culture can be mapped:
- Where is your culture on the evolution axis?
- Is your culture appropriate for the evolutionary stages of your work?

**The Culture-Evolution Mismatch:**
| Culture Type | Appropriate For | Inappropriate For |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Pioneer (explore, experiment) | Genesis/Custom work | Commodity operations |
| Settler (build, improve) | Custom→Product work | Extreme genesis or commodity |
| Town Planner (process, scale) | Product/Commodity | Genesis innovation |

If your culture is pioneer-dominant but most of your work is commodity operations, you have a cultural mismatch — pioneers will be frustrated, commodity operations will be unreliable.

### The Culture Gap
Maps reveal when stated culture contradicts actual culture:
- "We value innovation" → Map shows 90% of investment in commodity maintenance
- "We are data-driven" → Map shows decisions made by HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion)
- "We empower teams" → Map shows all architectural decisions require CTO approval

These gaps destroy trust and create cynicism. Maps make them visible and force resolution.

### How Culture Creates Strategic Blindspots
Different cultural archetypes see the landscape differently:
- **Finance-dominant culture**: Sees only cost/revenue components; misses strategic positioning
- **Engineering-dominant culture**: Sees technical components clearly; misses user-facing value
- **Sales-dominant culture**: Sees customer-facing components; misses infrastructure risks
- **Operations-dominant culture**: Sees process clearly; misses evolution opportunities

Maps that incorporate all perspectives have fewer blindspots.

## Ethics and the Map

### Mapping Ethical Dimensions
Ethical considerations are not external to strategy — they're embedded in the landscape:
- **User harm**: What happens to users if a component fails? Is it mapped as a critical dependency?
- **Societal impact**: What second-order effects does your strategy have on others not on your map?
- **Asymmetric power**: If you weaponize a value chain component, who gets hurt?

### The ILC Ethics Question
The ILC model (commoditize ecosystem innovations) is legal but raises questions:
- Does it destroy ecosystem trust?
- Does it harm small businesses that built on your platform?
- Is "it's legal" sufficient ethical justification?

Wardley's position: ethics must be explicit in strategy. Ignoring ethical dimensions of gameplay does not make them disappear — it makes them dangerous surprises.

### Data Ethics in the Map
When mapping data components, include:
- Privacy implications of data collection
- Power asymmetries created by data accumulation
- What happens to users if the data is misused or breached

These are not CSR add-ons — they are map components that carry risk.

## The Authenticity Test

Maps can be used to test organizational authenticity:

### The Purpose Test
1. State your organization's purpose
2. Map your actual investment and activity
3. Compare: does the investment align with the purpose?

If 80% of your investment is in maintaining commodity infrastructure while your stated purpose is innovation, you are not an innovation company. You are a maintenance company with an innovation aspiration.

### The Values Test
1. State your organizational values
2. Map the actual decisions made when values were tested
3. Compare: when cost reduction conflicts with user experience, what wins?

The map shows what you actually value, not what you aspire to value.

## Wardley's Own Biases

Wardley acknowledges his framework has biases:
- **Technology determinism**: The framework assumes evolution is driven by technology and market forces; political and cultural forces that can slow or redirect evolution are underweighted
- **Western capitalist context**: The framework assumes competitive markets; applies less cleanly to public sector or non-profit contexts
- **Large-scale applicability**: Maps work best for mid-to-large organizations with complex value chains; may be over-engineered for small businesses

### Using Wardley Maps in Non-Market Contexts
Government and public sector applications require modifications:
- The X-axis still works (components evolve)
- The Y-axis needs redefining (visibility to citizen/service user)
- "Evolution" in public sector is often driven by policy rather than competition
- Inertia in government is even more powerful (see: Chapter 18)

## Building Self-Awareness as an Organization

### The Doctrine Assessment as Self-Awareness Tool
Use the doctrine assessment (Chapter 4) to honestly evaluate where your organization is:
- Where do we systematically underperform?
- What doctrinal principles do we consistently violate?
- What cultural beliefs prevent us from improving?

### The Map as Mirror
Create a map not to be "right" but to see clearly:
- What does the map show about our actual position?
- What is the map revealing about our assumptions?
- What are we not willing to put on the map?

The components that people resist adding to the map are often the most strategically important.

### Psychological Safety and Maps
Maps only work as tools for honest assessment if:
- People can challenge others' component placements without political risk
- Leaders are willing to have their assumptions challenged
- Being wrong about a component position is treated as learning, not failure

Without psychological safety, maps become political artifacts — showing what people want to believe rather than what is true.

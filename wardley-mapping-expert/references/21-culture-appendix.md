# What Culture Is Right for You? — The Culture Appendix

Reference for Wardley's treatment of organizational culture: how culture evolves, why culture is multi-component, and how it relates to doctrine and organizational structure.

## The Core Argument: Culture is Not One Thing

Most frameworks treat culture as a unified variable — "our culture is X." Wardley challenges this:

> "Culture has many components rather than being one thing. It is a construct built on constructs."

This means asking "what culture is right for you?" is a poorly formed question. The right question is: "which cultural components are appropriate for which parts of your organization, given where those components sit on the evolutionary axis?"

## Cultural Values Evolve

Just as technology components evolve from genesis to commodity, so do values within organizations:

### The Four Stages of Cultural Evolution

**Stage I: Espoused Value**
- Explicitly stated as an ideal: "We value innovation"
- Not yet embedded in behavior or decision-making
- Exists in vision documents and leadership speeches
- Analogous to Genesis — novel, aspirational, not yet reliable

**Stage II: Emerging Theme**
- Values begin to emerge as recurring themes in how decisions are made
- People start referencing the value when justifying actions
- Still inconsistently applied
- Analogous to Custom — developing, not yet systematic

**Stage III: Forming Assumption**
- Conflict between emerging themes creates pressure toward resolution
- The value becomes an assumption underlying decisions (often implicit)
- "Of course we would do X — that's how we do things here"
- Analogous to Product — recognizable, teachable, variable quality

**Stage IV: Unconscious Norm**
- Value is taken for granted; no longer consciously stated
- Violating it feels wrong at a gut level
- New hires absorb it without being told
- Analogous to Commodity — invisible infrastructure, just how things work

**The implication**: When leaders say "we need to change our culture," they are often asking Stage IV norms to rapidly become Stage I aspirations for a different value — and back to Stage IV. This is extremely hard and slow.

## Organizational Culture Varies by Component

Different parts of an organization will have different cultural stages and different appropriate cultures:

### Pioneer Culture (appropriate for Genesis/Custom work)
- High tolerance for ambiguity and failure
- Celebration of experimentation and novel thinking
- Flat hierarchy and rapid decision-making
- Intense, often chaotic
- Uncomfortable for people who value process and certainty

### Settler Culture (appropriate for Custom→Product work)
- Appreciation for craft and quality
- Collaborative and iterative
- More structured than pioneer, less rigid than town planner
- Values both the novel and the reliable

### Town Planner Culture (appropriate for Product/Commodity work)
- Deep appreciation for process and consistency
- Low tolerance for unnecessary change
- Reliability and predictability are values, not constraints
- Find meaning in scaling and optimization
- Uncomfortable for people who value novelty

## The Empirical Evidence: Web Giant vs. Banking Giant

Wardley compared two organizations using doctrine assessments:

**Web Giant** profile:
- Strong on: transparency, situational awareness, small autonomous teams, open source use
- Weaker on: formal governance structures, regulatory compliance practices
- Cultural driver: speed, experimentation, platform thinking

**Banking Giant** profile:
- Strong on: regulatory compliance, formal governance, risk management
- Weaker on: small teams, rapid iteration, situational awareness
- Cultural driver: reliability, risk reduction, regulatory compliance

Neither is categorically better. The appropriate profile depends on:
- The evolutionary stage of their core business activities
- The regulatory environment
- The nature of the user needs they're serving

The web giant's culture applied to banking would be catastrophic. The banking giant's culture applied to a fast-moving technology company would be equally catastrophic.

## Culture Cannot Be Benchmarked Against Absolute Standards

This is Wardley's central point about culture:

There is no universally "good" culture. Culture is:
- **Contextual**: What works in one landscape fails in another
- **Emergent**: Shaped by the organization's history and competitive pressures
- **Historically contingent**: Past successes and failures leave cultural imprints
- **Multi-component**: No single culture characterizes an organization; different components have different cultures

**The mistake**: Companies attend "culture talks" by successful tech companies and try to copy their culture. But the culture is an *output* of their landscape position and history — not a transferable asset.

**The right approach**: Map your landscape. Understand what evolutionary stages your activities are in. Design cultural components appropriate to each stage. Build the PST structures that allow different cultures to coexist.

## Doctrine as Cultural Infrastructure

Doctrine (the universal principles from Chapter 4) represents cultural values that have evolved to Stage III/IV across many organizations:
- "Focus on user needs" — organizations that practice this long enough internalize it as an unconscious norm
- "Use a common language" — becomes infrastructure for communication without conscious effort
- "Be transparent" — becomes the default, not an explicit policy

Doctrine principles are the *most evolved* cultural elements — they are the Stage IV norms that transcend specific organizational contexts and apply universally.

## Practical Application

### Diagnosing Cultural Misalignment
1. Map your organization's activities to evolutionary stages
2. Assess the cultural characteristics of each team/group
3. Identify mismatches (Town Planner culture on Genesis work; Pioneer culture on Commodity operations)
4. Prioritize corrections based on strategic impact

### Building Cultural Change
Acknowledge that cultural evolution is slow:
- Stage I → Stage II: months to years
- Stage II → Stage III: years
- Stage III → Stage IV: decades

Shortcuts don't work. You cannot announce your way to a new culture. You must create the structural conditions (PST, incentives, hiring, governance) that allow the new cultural components to emerge naturally.

### Protecting Cultural Diversity
In a PST organization, actively protect the cultural differences between Pioneer, Settler, and Town Planner groups:
- Don't impose uniform culture across all three
- Celebrate that these groups are different — they're supposed to be
- Manage the interfaces between them (how Pioneer work becomes Settler work) rather than trying to make everyone the same

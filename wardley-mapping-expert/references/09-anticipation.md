# Anticipation — Predicting Change and Using Weak Signals

Reference for anticipating landscape changes, identifying weak signals, and using patterns to predict future evolution.

## The Power of Anticipation

Most organizations react to change. Some organizations anticipate change. The difference in outcomes is enormous.

Anticipation is possible because:
1. Climatic patterns are predictable (everything evolves)
2. The trajectory of evolution is visible on maps
3. Weak signals of future change are detectable before they become obvious

> "The future is already here — it's just not evenly distributed." — William Gibson

Maps make Gibson's observation actionable: you can see where different parts of the landscape are at different evolutionary stages and predict when the rest of the landscape will catch up.

## The Peace, War, and Wonder Cycle

One of Wardley's most important and original frameworks: economic competition cycles through three distinct states.

### The Three States

**Wonder** (Genesis stage)
- A novel activity appears with uncertain but potentially high future value
- High risk, exploratory, speculative
- Few players, no standards, high cost
- *"Isn't this wonderful — I wonder if it could be used for…"*

**Peace** (Product/Custom stage)
- Gradual evolution, product competition, feature differentiation
- High margins, relative stability between established competitors
- Slow movement toward commodity
- *"We compete on features and brand"*

**War** (Product→Commodity transition)
- Disruptive punctuated equilibrium — rapid, discontinuous market transition
- New utility entrants dominate; past giants face existential disruption
- Industry transformation: 20–30 years to build products, 10–15 years to dismantle
- Widespread, fast adoption; incumbent margins collapse
- *"Everything changes, and changes fast"*

After War, the new commodity layer enables new Wonder-stage activities, and the cycle begins again at a higher level.

### Four Conditions for State Change
For a component to transition (especially to utility), all four must exist:
1. **Concept**: The idea and approach must exist
2. **Technology**: Capability to build/deliver must be available
3. **Suitability**: Activity must be widespread, well-understood, and high-demand
4. **Attitude**: Consumer willingness driven by dissatisfaction with the status quo

Use this checklist to assess whether a component is *about* to shift — all four present = shift imminent.

### Predictability Categories

Not all changes are equally predictable. Wardley classifies anticipatable change into three categories:

| Category | Definition | Strategic Value |
|----------|------------|----------------|
| **Known** | Obvious existing trends — everyone can see them | Minimal advantage; table stakes |
| **Unknown** | Genuinely unpredictable changes (e.g., which specific product substitutes another) | Cannot anticipate; manage with optionality |
| **Knowable** | Determinable trends considered unknown by most | **Highest advantage** — this is where mapping pays off |

The Knowable category is why Wardley Mapping exists. Product-to-utility transitions are predictable through maps; most organizations treat them as unknown.

### The Notation System
- **p(what)**: Predictability of the *nature* of the change
- **p(when)**: Predictability of the *timing* of the change

Product-to-utility shifts: high p(what) — we know *what* will happen. Low p(when) — timing is uncertain.

### Key Economic Principles (from Chapter 9)
- "The future value of something is inversely related to the certainty we have over it" — genesis components have high potential value because they are uncertain
- "Success breeds inertia" — past achievement creates resistance to evolution
- "Inertia kills" — companies fail from business model resistance, not from lack of innovation
- "Efficiency enables innovation" — componentization effects allow new higher-order activities
- "Capital flows to new areas of value" during product-to-utility transitions
- **The Red Queen Effect**: continuous adaptation required just to maintain position

## The Three Horizons of Change

For any component on your map, there are three relevant timeframes:

### Horizon 1: The Present (0–18 months)
- Current competitive dynamics
- Existing products and services
- Known risks and opportunities
- Manage and optimize

### Horizon 2: Emerging (18 months – 3 years)
- Components currently in transition (moving rightward on map)
- New entrants using evolved forms of key components
- Practices co-evolving with technology
- Invest and experiment

### Horizon 3: Future (3–10 years)
- Genesis-stage innovations that will eventually mature
- Fundamental shifts in landscape enabled by current commoditization
- New user needs emerging from evolved landscape
- Explore and monitor

**Maps allow you to populate all three horizons** by looking at where components currently sit and extrapolating their trajectory.

## Weak Signal Detection

Weak signals are early indicators of landscape change that are visible before they become mainstream trends.

### Where to Find Weak Signals

**1. Academic Research**
- Today's academic papers are tomorrow's products
- Monitor research in areas adjacent to your value chain
- Patent filings show where incumbents are hedging

**2. Startup Activity**
- Startups cluster around evolving opportunities
- Many startups attacking the same space = that space is evolving
- VC funding patterns reveal where investors see evolution happening

**3. Open Source Activity**
- Open source projects often appear at the transition from Custom to Product
- High-activity open source projects in your space = that component is productizing
- When open source appears, commodity is 5–10 years away

**4. Government and Regulation**
- Regulations often follow (and codify) evolutionary changes
- Government adoption of a technology signals late Product / early Commodity
- Watch where governments are being forced to define standards

**5. Pricing Changes**
- Dramatic price drops signal commoditization
- The cloud pricing wars (2010–2020) signaled commodity compute
- When prices drop 10x, a component is commoditizing

**6. Language Changes**
- When a component gets a generic name, it's commoditizing
- "The cloud" replacing "hosted servers" signals commodity
- When MBA programs add a topic to their curriculum, that topic is Product-stage

**7. Talent Movement**
- Where are senior engineers moving?
- What skills are becoming scarce?
- What skills are becoming commoditized (abundant, cheap)?

## Pattern-Based Anticipation

### Using Climatic Patterns as Predictions

Each climatic pattern is a prediction machine:

**"Everything evolves"** →
*Predict:* Anything currently in Custom/Product will commoditize. Build now to be ready.

**"Past success breeds inertia"** →
*Predict:* The most successful incumbents will be the last to adapt. Disruption will come from attackers with less to lose.

**"Efficiency enables innovation"** →
*Predict:* When compute commoditized → cloud apps exploded. When cloud apps commoditize → next wave of innovation will emerge from that base.

**"Co-evolution of practices"** →
*Predict:* When a technology shifts, the associated practices will shift 2–5 years later.

### The S-Curve and Evolutionary Timing

Each component follows an S-curve of adoption:

```
Adoption
   ↑
100%│                    ╭──────────
   │                ╭──╯
   │             ╭─╯
   │          ╭─╯
   │      ╭──╯
   │  ╭──╯
  0%└──────────────────────→ Time
     Genesis  Custom  Product  Commodity
```

**Reading the S-curve for anticipation:**
- If a component is in the *early* part of the S-curve (Genesis/Custom), it will look unimpressive — this is the time to invest
- If a component is at the *inflection point* (Custom→Product transition), this is the highest-risk/highest-reward moment
- If a component is *past the inflection* (Product→Commodity), it's too late to build a position from scratch

### Anticipating the Transition Points

The most important moments to anticipate:

**Custom → Product transition:**
- First commercial product versions appear
- Venture funding accelerates
- First industry conferences emerge
- First Gartner coverage appears
- **Action**: Decide whether to build a product or buy the emerging products

**Product → Commodity transition:**
- AWS-style utility pricing appears
- Open source alternatives reach production quality
- Price drops become dramatic
- **Action**: Stop investing in differentiation here; redirect to what's above it on the value chain

## Anticipating Competitor Behavior

Maps enable competitive anticipation:

### Mapping Competitor Inertia
Place competitors on your map. Ask:
- Where have they invested heavily? (This is where their inertia is highest)
- What components are evolving that they are most committed to?
- Which evolutionary transitions will hurt them most?

**The answer**: The place where your competitor has the most inertia is the most attractive place for you to attack.

### Predicting Competitor Response
When you make a move, anticipate how competitors will respond:
- Their response will be constrained by their inertia
- They will defend their highest-margin, most-committed positions
- They will be slow to cannibalize their own revenue

This predictability is your advantage.

## The Danger of Being Too Early

Anticipation can fail in one specific way: being right but too early.

### The Early Mover Curse
- You build for a future that hasn't arrived yet
- You spend resources educating the market rather than serving it
- Followers enter after you've done the hard work and beat you with better execution on the established path

**How to manage it:**
- Distinguish between "this will happen" and "this will happen now"
- Place small bets early; scale when signals confirm the timing
- Maps show trajectory but not exact speed — maintain optionality

### Indicators of Timing

| Signal | Suggests |
|--------|---------|
| Startups raising Series B/C | 2–5 years to mainstream |
| Enterprise early adopters buying | 1–3 years to mainstream |
| Government pilots | 3–7 years to mainstream |
| Open source v1.0 | 5–10 years to commodity |
| Gartner Hype Cycle "peak" | 3–7 years to productization |

## Building Anticipation Capability

### Environmental Monitoring System
Create a systematic way to monitor your landscape:
1. **Map your landscape** (current state)
2. **Identify the edges** — what is appearing at genesis stage that could affect your value chain?
3. **Set up monitoring** — who is responsible for watching each edge?
4. **Regular map reviews** — quarterly reviews to update component positions
5. **Scenario planning** — for 2–3 significant transitions, plan responses now

### The "Pre-Mortem" for Anticipation
Imagine it's 5 years from now and your business has been disrupted. Work backward:
- What component evolved unexpectedly?
- Which competitor made a move you didn't anticipate?
- Which climatic pattern triggered the change?

This exercise surfaces the most likely anticipation failures.

## Weak Signals vs. Noise

Not every signal represents real evolution. Distinguish:

**Likely signal** (evolution happening):
- Multiple independent actors responding to the same opportunity
- Customer behavior shifting (not just customer saying what they want)
- Pricing pressure appearing from new entrants
- Developer community energy shifting toward new approaches

**Likely noise** (hype without substance):
- Single high-profile announcement
- Analyst report without evidence
- Media coverage without underlying customer adoption
- Vendor marketing

The map is your filter: ask "does this signal fit the evolutionary trajectory I'd expect for this component?"

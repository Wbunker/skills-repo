# Finding a New Purpose — Why Strategy Matters

Reference for the role of purpose, mission, and vision in Wardley Mapping, and how maps enable better organizational purpose.

## The Problem with Mission and Vision Statements

Most mission and vision statements are:
- Aspirational but not actionable
- Disconnected from the actual landscape
- Used as PR rather than navigation tools
- Identical to competitors ("be the leading provider of...")

They cannot answer the fundamental strategic questions:
- What do we focus on?
- What do we stop doing?
- How do we allocate scarce resources?
- What is our actual position in the landscape?

> "A mission statement without a map is just poetry."

## Purpose in the Context of Wardley Mapping

Purpose operates at three levels:

### 1. Moral Purpose (Why we exist)
- The reason the organization exists beyond making money
- What user need are we ultimately serving?
- What would be worse in the world if we didn't exist?

This is the anchor for the map — it determines what goes on the map.

### 2. Strategic Purpose (How we compete)
- What is our approach to creating value in our landscape?
- Where are we choosing to play?
- What capabilities are we building or acquiring?

This is informed by the map — you can only have a coherent strategic purpose if you understand your landscape.

### 3. Operational Purpose (What we do day-to-day)
- How do we deliver on the strategic purpose?
- What processes, systems, and practices do we use?
- This is managed through doctrine

## The Five Factors of Strategic Success

From Sun Tzu, applied through Wardley's lens:

### 1. Moral Law (Purpose and Culture)
Do your people believe in what they're doing? Are they aligned with the organization's purpose?
- Misalignment between stated purpose and actual behavior destroys trust
- Maps can reveal when actual investment patterns contradict stated purpose

### 2. Heaven (Timing)
Are you moving with the right timing? Too early = waste; too late = irrelevance.
- Climate patterns determine timing
- Maps show where components are in their evolution → when to act

### 3. Earth (Landscape)
Do you understand your terrain? Do you know where you are?
- This is what Wardley Maps provide directly
- Without landscape awareness, all purpose is aspirational at best

### 4. Commander (Leadership and Doctrine)
Are your leaders capable? Does your organization have good doctrinal fitness?
- Doctrine (Chapter 4) is the commander's toolkit
- Good purpose requires capable execution

### 5. Method and Discipline (Execution)
Can you actually do what you intend?
- Systems, processes, practices
- Organizational structure aligned to the map

## Mapping as a Purpose-Clarifying Tool

### Revealing Contradictions
A map often reveals that an organization's stated purpose conflicts with its actual behavior:

**Example:**
- Stated purpose: "We are a technology innovator"
- Map reveals: 90% of engineering effort is on maintaining commodity components
- Reality: "We are a technology maintenance organization that occasionally innovates"

The map is more honest than the mission statement.

### Enabling Informed Purpose
Once you have a map, you can have a meaningful conversation about purpose:
- "Given where our landscape is evolving, what *can* we realistically be good at?"
- "What would we need to invest in to become the innovation leader we claim to be?"
- "Is our stated purpose achievable, and what would it cost?"

### Aligning Resources with Purpose
Maps enable the resource allocation conversation that mission statements cannot:
- If purpose is innovation, resources should go to genesis-stage components
- If purpose is reliability, resources should go to commodity excellence
- If purpose is disruption, identify and exploit inertia in the map

## The Problem of Copying Without Purpose

A common failure pattern:
1. Company A succeeds with strategy X
2. Company B observes and copies strategy X
3. Company B fails because:
   - Their landscape is different
   - The timing is different
   - Company A had doctrine Company B lacks
   - Company A had a purpose that drove the strategy; Company B just copied the tactics

Purpose must emerge from understanding of your own landscape, not from benchmarking competitors.

## Finding Strategic Intent

### The Right Questions
Instead of "What is our vision?" ask:
1. What does our map show about where we currently are?
2. What is the landscape telling us about where things are going?
3. Given that, what position could we credibly occupy?
4. What capabilities would we need to build or acquire?
5. What do we need to stop doing to free up resources for this?

### The Three Strategic Positions
Maps reveal three broad strategic positions:

**1. Attacker** (disruptor)
- Find incumbents with high inertia
- Move to the evolved form of their core capability
- Commoditize what they're selling
- Build on top of the commodity you've created

**2. Defender** (incumbent)
- Identify what is evolving toward you
- Manage inertia consciously
- Use ecosystem plays to create moats
- Transition revenue to new models before the cliff

**3. Settler** (platform player)
- Identify where multiple industries are converging
- Create platforms at the intersection
- Play ILC (Innovate, Leverage, Commoditize)

## Organizational Purpose and the Map

### Connecting Purpose to Organizational Structure
Your organizational structure should reflect your strategic purpose:
- Purpose: innovation → Pioneer-heavy structure
- Purpose: scale → Town Planner-heavy structure
- Purpose: platform → all three in balance

### The Gap Analysis
Use maps to identify the gap between current state and desired purpose:
- Where are you now?
- Where does your purpose require you to be?
- What stands between here and there?
- What would it cost to close the gap?

This gap analysis turns abstract purpose into concrete investment and organizational change decisions.

## Warning: Purpose as Manipulation

Wardley warns against using purpose cynically — as a tool to manipulate employees or customers. Maps can expose this too.

If an organization's map shows it is harvesting a declining product but its stated purpose is innovation, this creates cognitive dissonance in employees who can see the reality. Authentic purpose must be grounded in genuine intent, and maps make inauthenticity visible.

## Summary: Purpose + Map + Action

The complete picture:

```
Purpose (Why)
    ↓
Map (Where you are, where things are going)
    ↓
Strategy (What moves to make, given the map)
    ↓
Doctrine (How to execute)
    ↓
Gameplay (Specific moves in the landscape)
    ↓
Measurement (Did it work? Update the map)
```

Purpose without a map is aspiration. Map without purpose is a navigation tool with no destination. Together, they enable deliberate, grounded strategy.

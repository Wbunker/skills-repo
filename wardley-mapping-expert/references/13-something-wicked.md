# Something Wicked This Way Comes — Disruption and Second-Order Effects

Reference for understanding disruption patterns, the ILC model, and second and third-order effects of evolutionary change.

## What Makes Disruption "Wicked"

Disruption from the incumbent's perspective is:
- Often visible in advance (on the map)
- Structurally impossible to respond to while protecting existing revenue
- Accelerated by the incumbent's own inertia
- More complete and faster than incumbents expect

The "wickedness" is not randomness — it's the combination of predictable patterns and structural inability to respond.

## The ILC Model in Detail

**ILC = Innovate, Leverage, Commoditize**

This is the most powerful platform strategy and the most dangerous threat to non-platform players.

### How ILC Works

```
Phase 1: Innovate
  Ecosystem members build new things on your platform
  → You observe what gets traction
  → You learn what users actually want

Phase 2: Leverage
  Identify the innovations gaining adoption
  → Learn from ecosystem member experiences
  → Understand unit economics before you invest

Phase 3: Commoditize
  Take the successful innovations
  → Build them into your core platform
  → Price them at utility (commodity) levels
  → Ecosystem members who built that feature become uncompetitive
```

### Why ILC is Devastating for Ecosystem Members
- You build a business on the platform
- Platform observes your success
- Platform copies your core feature
- Platform prices it at zero or near-zero
- Your business model is destroyed

**Examples:**
- Amazon 3rd-party sellers → Amazon Basics products
- iOS App Store apps → Apple features (podcasts, night mode, password manager)
- AWS startups → AWS services (Elastic, Redis → AWS managed equivalents)

### Why ILC is Sustainable for Platforms
- Free R&D from ecosystem innovation
- Low risk (only commoditize what's proven)
- Increasing platform value → more ecosystem members → more innovation to leverage

## Second-Order Effects

When a component evolves, it causes changes far beyond the immediate substitution.

### The Electricity Analogy
When electricity became a utility:
- **First-order effect**: Gas lighting companies declined
- **Second-order effect**: Electric appliance industry created
- **Third-order effect**: Urban planning changed (no need for horse infrastructure)
- **Fourth-order effect**: New work patterns emerged (factories could be lit at night)
- **Fifth-order effect**: Consumer culture emerged (appliances as status symbols)

The first-order effect (gas lighting companies failing) was obvious. The fifth-order effect (consumer culture) was not.

### The Cloud Second-Order Effects
When compute became commodity:
- **First-order**: On-premise data centers became obsolete
- **Second-order**: Startup capital requirements dropped 100x → startup explosion
- **Third-order**: Software-as-a-service became the default business model
- **Fourth-order**: SaaS multiplied data exhaust → ML became practical at scale
- **Fifth-order**: AI capabilities commoditizing → [next disruption cycle begins]

### Mapping Second-Order Effects
To anticipate second-order effects:
1. Identify what becomes possible when a component commoditizes
2. Map the new value chains those possibilities enable
3. Identify who is currently positioned to exploit those possibilities
4. Identify who will be disrupted by those new value chains

## The Disruption Patterns

### Pattern 1: The Unbundling
When a bundle of components (sold as one product) gets unbundled by specialists:

**Classic example**: The newspaper
- News + Classifieds + Entertainment + Sports + Jobs = Bundle
- Craigslist took classifieds; LinkedIn took jobs; Facebook took entertainment; Google took news discovery
- The bundle was destroyed one component at a time

**Map interpretation**: When you see a bundle where individual components are at different evolutionary stages, unbundling is coming. The components furthest along will be attacked first.

### Pattern 2: The Rebundling
After unbundling, winners rebundle at a new layer:

- First: Google took news search
- Then: Google rebundled news + maps + email + videos + documents
- Now: Google is a new bundle at a different layer

**Map interpretation**: After unbundling, look for who will rebundle at the newly commoditized layer. That player will be the next dominant incumbent.

### Pattern 3: The Substitution
A component is replaced entirely by an evolved alternative:

- SMS → WhatsApp (substitution at the commodity layer)
- Physical retail → e-commerce (substitution of the delivery mechanism)
- Landlines → mobile (substitution of the access layer)

**Map interpretation**: When a component is at Product stage and a new component emerges at Genesis that could perform the same function with 10x better economics, plan for substitution.

### Pattern 4: The New Market
Commoditization creates new markets that didn't exist:

- When cloud commoditized compute, the IoT market became viable
- When smartphones commoditized GPS, location-based services emerged
- When sequencing commoditized genomics, personalized medicine became possible

**Map interpretation**: Map the new value chains made possible by each commoditization. The highest-value opportunities are often not in the commoditizing component itself but in the new layer above it.

## The Speed of Disruption

Disruption is consistently underestimated in speed:

### Why Disruption is Always Faster Than Expected
1. **Adoption curves accelerate**: Each new technology deploys faster than the last (internet took 20 years to reach 50% adoption; smartphones took 7; social media took 3)
2. **Ecosystem amplification**: Platforms amplify adoption through network effects
3. **Incumbent paralysis**: Inertia grows as disruption approaches (perversely)
4. **Switching cost collapse**: As alternatives mature, switching costs fall

### The Cassandra Problem
Mappers who see disruption coming often cannot be believed:
- The evidence is visible but not mainstream
- The incumbent has strong incentives to discount the signal
- "This time is different" — incumbents always believe their situation is unique

**Solution**: Use the map as objective evidence. Show the trajectory, not just the threat.

## Ecosystem Disruption

### How Ecosystems Get Disrupted
Ecosystems are more resilient than single-product companies — but they can be disrupted through their platform:

1. Identify the platform component that the ecosystem depends on
2. Commoditize that platform component
3. The entire ecosystem must adapt or rebuild

**Example**: When Google changed its search algorithm (a platform component), entire SEO industries were disrupted — not because their practices changed but because the platform component they depended on changed.

### Designing Disruption-Resistant Ecosystems
- Create value at multiple points in the ecosystem, not just the platform layer
- Ensure ecosystem members have interests aligned with the platform's evolution
- Anticipate platform commoditization and build your position one layer higher

## Using Disruption Maps

### The Disruption Analysis Template
For any industry you want to analyze:

1. **Map the current industry value chain**
2. **Identify which components are evolving** (moving rightward)
3. **Identify which players have high inertia** (incumbents with most to lose)
4. **Identify which components will commoditize next** (and what becomes possible above them)
5. **Identify the new value chains** enabled by those commoditizations
6. **Identify who is positioned to exploit them** (new entrants without inertia)

### Who Will Win
In a disruption:
- **Attackers without inertia** in the disrupted component
- **Platform players** who can ILC the disruption
- **Incumbents who anticipate early** and manage their own inertia

### Who Will Lose
- **Incumbents with deep inertia** in the disrupting component
- **Ecosystem members** of disrupted platforms
- **Specialists** in the disrupted component who cannot pivot

## The Warning Signs of Incoming Disruption

If you see these on your map, disruption is likely within 5 years:

1. A genesis-stage alternative exists that performs the core function of a key component
2. That alternative is cheaper by more than 5x
3. Early adopters are using it despite its immaturity
4. The enabling infrastructure (at the bottom of your map) is commoditizing
5. Startups are raising capital to attack the space
6. Your best engineers are expressing interest in the new approach

## Preparing for Disruption

Rather than asking "will we be disrupted?" (answer: yes), ask:
- When will we be disrupted?
- Which components will be disrupted first?
- What will we build on top of the commodity that emerges?
- How do we manage the transition without destroying current value?

Maps make this conversation concrete, specific, and actionable — instead of abstract and fearful.

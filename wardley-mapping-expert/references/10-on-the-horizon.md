# On the Horizon — Future Trends and Co-Evolution

Reference for horizon scanning, co-evolution of practices and technology, and preparing for landscape shifts.

## What Lies Beyond the Current Map

Every map is a snapshot. The landscape is always moving. "On the Horizon" refers to the changes visible in the near distance — the genesis-stage components that will, in time, reshape your entire value chain.

## The Three Co-Evolving Elements

When a technology evolves, it doesn't evolve alone. Three elements always co-evolve:

### 1. Technology / Component
The core component itself — moving from genesis through custom to commodity.

### 2. Practices
How people use and manage the component changes as it evolves.

**Example: Compute Evolution**
| Stage | Technology | Practices |
|-------|------------|----------|
| Genesis | Early mainframes | Custom programming, specialists only |
| Custom | Mini-computers | Assembly language, IT departments |
| Product | PCs / servers | Windows sysadmin, ITIL processes |
| Commodity | Cloud | DevOps, Infrastructure as Code, SRE |

The practices couldn't exist before the technology matured. And the technology's maturation was *enabled* by the new practices.

### 3. Data / Knowledge
What we know about the component grows as it evolves. The data generated by commodity-scale deployment enables entirely new forms of intelligence.

**Example: Payment Evolution**
- Genesis: Cash transactions, manual records
- Custom: Electronic ledgers, batch processing
- Product: Real-time processing, fraud rules
- Commodity: ML fraud detection on billions of transactions, behavioral analytics

The data patterns only become visible at commodity scale.

## Punctuated Equilibrium and Economic Ages

### Punctuated Equilibrium
Change *appears* gradual because of overlapping product improvements — but the shift from product to utility form is actually rapid and discontinuous.

**The "Marble Hall" Analogy:**
Imagine a marble hall filling with water. At 45 seconds, it's only 3% full. You think: no problem, plenty of time. Five seconds later — it's completely full. The exponential nature of adoption means organizations repeatedly underestimate speed of disruption.

### Kondratiev Waves and Carlotta Perez
Per economist Carlotta Perez: economic ages don't begin with *invention* but with *industrialization* of pre-existing activities.

- Electricity: ~1,400 years after the Parthian Battery
- Mechanical components: ~1,500 years after Archimedes' screw
- The internet: invented 1969 (ARPANET), industrialized ~2000–2010

**Implication for maps**: Don't look for new inventions — look for existing activities that are now ready to industrialize (all four conditions met: concept, technology, suitability, attitude).

### The Three Disruption Types
Not all disruption is equal:

| Type | Predictability | Example |
|------|---------------|---------|
| **Unpredictable genesis** | Cannot anticipate which new activities emerge | Blockchain, generative AI |
| **Unpredictable product substitution** | Cannot know which product replaces another | BlackBerry → Android (we knew smartphones would win, not Android specifically) |
| **Predictable product-to-utility shifts** | Highly predictable via maps | On-premise servers → cloud; licensed software → SaaS |

Maps give you an advantage only in the third type — but the third type is also the most economically significant (the Wars).

### The 2011 Leading Edge Forum Study
Research of 100+ Silicon Valley companies found statistically significant "next generation" organizational phenotypes — distinguishable from traditional organizations:

| Traditional | Next Generation |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Single development method | Mixed development methods |
| Scale-up architecture | Distributed systems |
| Departmental silos | Cell-based structures |
| Open strategies as cost reduction | Open strategies as competitive weapons |

These patterns were detectable *before* disruption made them obvious — a validation of the weak signal approach.

### The Death Spiral
A specific failure pattern during product-to-utility wars:

1. Incumbents face margin pressure from utility entrants
2. They cut costs to protect profitability
3. They cut the "radicals" and "pioneers" who see the future (these people are uncomfortable, questioning)
4. They retain the people who succeeded in the old model (optimized for declining business)
5. Adaptive capacity is destroyed just when it's most needed
6. The spiral accelerates

**The Inertia Kills Pattern**: Companies don't fail because they lack innovation — they fail because past success creates blinders. Kodak, Blockbuster, Nokia: not destroyed by lack of smart people, but by the gravitational pull of what had previously worked.

## Key Horizon Trends (From Wardley's Work)

### Trend 1: The Commoditization of AI
AI is moving through the evolution axis. The question is which components and at what speed.

**Current state (as of Wardley's framework):**
- ML frameworks: Product → Commodity transition
- Foundation models: Custom → Product transition
- AI applications: Genesis → Custom
- AI commodity utilities: Future

**Anticipation**: AI capabilities will commoditize. The competitive advantage will not be "having AI" — it will be what you do with the data the AI processes and what new activities become possible when AI is a utility.

### Trend 2: The Evolution of Organizational Practices
As technology commoditizes, organizational practices must evolve with it:

**Post-cloud organizational evolution:**
- Old: IT department runs infrastructure → New: Engineering teams own their infrastructure
- Old: Quarterly releases → New: Continuous deployment
- Old: IT project governance → New: Product-led development
- Old: Cost center IT → New: Engineering as competitive advantage

Organizations that adopted cloud without evolving their practices got the cost increase of cloud without the agility benefits.

### Trend 3: Data as Infrastructure
Data is evolving from a business asset to infrastructure:

```
Genesis: Data is a byproduct of operations
Custom: Data analytics as a specialized function
Product: Self-service BI, data platforms
Commodity: Data infrastructure (like electricity) — enabling a new layer of innovation
```

When data becomes infrastructure, new businesses become possible that could not previously exist.

### Trend 4: The Platform Layer
Every commoditization creates a new layer on which platforms can be built:

```
Layer 4: New genesis innovations (enabled by Layer 3)
Layer 3: Platforms built on commodity Layer 2
Layer 2: Commodity infrastructure (cloud, data, AI)
Layer 1: Physical infrastructure (networking, energy)
```

Being on the right layer matters enormously. Companies that commoditized Layer 2 (Amazon with cloud, Google with search) now have platform advantages that are very hard to attack.

## Co-Evolution in Practice: The DevOps Example

DevOps illustrates co-evolution perfectly:

**Technology evolution**: Virtualization → cloud compute
**Practice co-evolution**:
1. Cloud compute becomes available (technology)
2. Teams realize they can provision infrastructure faster than the old process allows (gap)
3. Infrastructure-as-Code emerges (practice innovation)
4. CI/CD pipelines become standard (practice productization)
5. DevOps becomes a defined discipline (practice commoditization)
6. SRE (Site Reliability Engineering) emerges as the next genesis practice

Each step enabled the next. And each step created new organizational requirements.

## The Horizon Scanning Process

### Step 1: Identify Your Value Chain Boundaries
What is at the bottom of your current map? What are you taking as "given"?

### Step 2: Ask "What is Below My Bottom?"
What components support the things you treat as givens? Those are on your horizon.

### Step 3: Watch for Evolution in Those Components
If the commodities you depend on are changing, your landscape is changing.

**Example**: A company building on top of cloud computing needs to monitor:
- AI commodity services (will they commoditize what you're building?)
- Networking evolution (edge computing could reshape what "cloud" means)
- Energy costs (data center economics affect cloud pricing)

### Step 4: Look for the Layer Above You
What becomes possible when your highest-value components become commodities?

If your core product will commoditize, what would someone build on top of a commoditized version of your product? That company is your future competitor.

## Detecting Emerging Practices

Practices emerge before they are named. Signals of an emerging practice:

1. **Blog posts describing "how we do it at X company"** → Practice emerging
2. **Conference talks about organizational experiments** → Practice early-adoption
3. **Job titles appearing** → Practice formalization
4. **Books published** → Practice productization
5. **University courses** → Practice nearing commodity
6. **Job descriptions treating it as table stakes** → Practice commodity

### Current Practice Evolution Examples
- **Platform Engineering**: Custom → Product (emerging as a distinct discipline)
- **FinOps**: Custom → Product (cloud cost management as a practice)
- **DataOps**: Genesis → Custom (data pipeline DevOps equivalent)
- **MLOps**: Custom → Product (ML operations as a practice)

## Preparing the Organization for Horizon Changes

### Build Sensing Capability
Designate people to monitor horizon trends:
- "Technology scouts" who track genesis-stage innovations
- Ecosystem monitors who watch startup and research activity
- Competitive analysts who track where competitors are investing

### Create Experimentation Capacity
Horizon trends need to be tested before they're mainstream:
- Small, funded experiments with emerging technologies
- Isolated teams with permission to fail
- Clear criteria for "when does this graduate to our mainstream map?"

### Manage the Transition
When a horizon trend reaches your map:
1. Create a new component on the map
2. Position it at its current evolutionary stage
3. Identify which existing components it will affect
4. Plan the transition

## The Danger of Horizon-Blindness

Organizations without horizon awareness:
- Are perpetually surprised by disruption
- React rather than anticipate
- Invest in the wrong stages of the right opportunities
- Find themselves defending commoditizing positions when they should be building on top of them

The map is only useful if it is regularly updated with horizon intelligence. A map of last year's landscape is a dangerous guide to next year's strategy.

## The 3-Year Rule

A rough heuristic: anything visible in academic research or early-stage startups today will be in your map within 3 years and will have a significant impact within 5.

Use this to:
- Set a 3-year planning horizon informed by current weak signals
- Identify 2–3 significant transitions to plan for
- Build option value — small investments that can be scaled if the trend materializes

# On the Practice of Scenario Planning — Role-Based Analysis and Strategic Variants

Reference for scenario planning with maps: role-based analysis, substitution variants, the spiral of death, and applying maps to specific strategic decisions.

## The Core Insight: Different Perspectives Have Different Maxima

A map doesn't prescribe a single right action. Different actors in the same landscape have different optimal strategies based on their roles, capabilities, and positions.

> "Different perspectives have different maxima in a single landscape."

This is why scenario planning matters: the same map supports multiple valid strategies, and understanding *your* role is as important as understanding the landscape.

## Role-Based Analysis

### Mapping Your Role
Before asking "what should we do?", ask:
- What is our role in this landscape?
- What position do we currently occupy?
- What capabilities do we have that others don't?
- What constraints do we face that competitors don't?

The same opportunity on a map means different things to:
- A **CEO** (organization-level position)
- A **hedge fund manager** (financial return optimization)
- A **consumer** (personal value maximization)
- A **government regulator** (systemic risk management)
- A **startup** (asymmetric upside with limited downside)

### Mapping Competitors' Roles
Equally important: understand what role *competitors* are playing:
- Are they an incumbent defending a commoditizing position?
- Are they a disruptor exploiting competitor inertia?
- Are they a platform player executing ILC?
- Are they a harvester extracting value from a declining product?

Understanding their role predicts their moves.

## Scenario Variants

Chapter 15 specifically analyzes two substitution patterns that require different strategic responses:

### Variant 1: Product-to-Product Substitution
**What it is**: One product replaces another (e.g., BlackBerry → Android, Myspace → Facebook)

**Characteristics:**
- The component stage doesn't change (still Product)
- Competition is about features, UX, ecosystem
- Margins remain reasonable for the winner
- The underlying infrastructure doesn't shift

**Strategic options:**
- **Second Curve Strategy**: Identify and invest in the product that will do the substituting before it becomes obvious
- **Platform Play**: Position in the layer that survives both products (the distribution layer, the data layer)
- **Feature Race**: If you're the current product, out-innovate the challenger while you have resources

### Variant 2: Product-to-Utility Substitution
**What it is**: A product category is replaced by a utility/commodity form (e.g., on-premise servers → cloud, licensed software → SaaS)

**Characteristics:**
- The component shifts from Product to Commodity stage
- Margins collapse; price competition dominates
- The entire ecosystem around the product changes
- All the practices and data co-evolve with the shift

**Strategic options:**
- **Sweating the Legacy**: If you have no realistic path to utility, maximize returns from the product business while it has value — but plan the exit
- **Utility Transition**: Move to the utility model faster than competitors; accept margin sacrifice for position
- **Layer Up**: Build on top of the commoditizing layer; let others fight the commodity war while you capture the higher-order value

## The Spiral of Death

A critical failure pattern that occurs when incumbents try to respond to utility substitution:

### The Spiral Pattern
1. Utility entrant creates margin pressure on the incumbent product
2. Incumbent cuts costs to protect earnings
3. Crucially: the cuts target "radicals," pioneers, and uncomfortable questioners (who have low perceived value in the current model)
4. The retained people are those who succeeded in the old model (high inertia)
5. Adaptive capacity is destroyed at exactly the moment it's most needed
6. The spiral accelerates — each round of cuts removes more adaptive capacity

### Why the Spiral is Common
Cost-cutting is locally rational at each step:
- "These people aren't delivering revenue"
- "We can't afford experiments when margins are under pressure"
- "Let's focus on the core business"

But the sum of locally rational decisions is organizational death.

### Breaking the Spiral
To break the spiral, you must:
1. **Identify which people ARE the adaptive capacity** — often not obvious
2. **Protect innovation capacity explicitly** — treat it as infrastructure, not discretionary
3. **Create a separate structure** for the utility transition, funded separately from the harvesting business

## The Tower and Moat in Scenario Planning

When a utility competitor is commoditizing your product category, they use a specific pattern:

**Tower**: The utility service itself (simple, reliable, cheap)
**Moat**: The ecosystem of innovations built on top of the utility

The utility competitor copies features from product competitors (neutralizing differentiation), builds them into the utility, and prices at commodity levels. Each copy reduces the product competitor's advantage.

**Response**: You cannot fight the tower by matching features (they price at zero). You must:
- Build your moat higher (proprietary data, deep integrations, unique capabilities)
- Or transition to the utility and build a new tower above it

## Applying the LFP Framework

Chapter 15 presents a scenario requiring decision-making between:
- **In-house variant**: Build and own the infrastructure
- **Public platform variant**: Build on top of someone else's utility infrastructure

**The analysis framework:**
1. Map the landscape (current positions, evolution trajectories)
2. Identify the substitution variant in play (product-to-product or product-to-utility?)
3. Model financial outcomes for each variant (probability × return)
4. Consider co-evolution effects (what practices change? what skills become needed?)
5. Evaluate inertia in each path (which path creates more lock-in?)
6. Decide based on strategic position, not just near-term financials

> "Maps simply provide a systematic way of learning, of not repeating old mistakes, of applying patterns from one context to another."

## Mapping as a Practice, Not an Event

The value of Wardley Mapping comes from doing it regularly, not once. Like any practice:
- First maps are rough and valuable
- Maps improve with feedback and iteration
- The process of building maps teaches you more than the finished map
- Organizational mapping capability compounds over time

## Levels of Mapping Expertise

### Level 1: Can Create a Map
- Understands the two axes
- Can identify users, needs, and components
- Can roughly place components on the evolution axis
- Creates useful (if imperfect) maps

### Level 2: Can Read a Map
- Can identify patterns (mismatches, bottlenecks, opportunities)
- Can reason about what the map implies for decisions
- Can challenge component placements with evidence
- Can communicate map insights to non-mappers

### Level 3: Can Use Maps for Strategy
- Translates map patterns into concrete moves
- Anticipates climatic pattern impacts
- Identifies and evaluates available gameplay
- Builds maps collaboratively with diverse stakeholders

### Level 4: Can Teach and Scale Mapping
- Facilitates effective mapping workshops
- Creates a shared mapping language in an organization
- Helps others develop mapping instincts
- Maintains a living map ecosystem for the organization

## Advanced Map Critique

### The Five Questions for Map Review

**1. Is the anchor correct?**
- Is the map oriented around a real user with a real need?
- Could the anchor be wrong? (e.g., mapping for an assumed user rather than an actual one)

**2. Is the value chain complete?**
- Are there dependencies missing?
- Are there components that exist but aren't on the map?
- Could someone outside the team identify gaps?

**3. Are the positions defensible?**
- For each component: what evidence supports its evolutionary position?
- Where are the positions most contested?
- What would it take to move a component's position?

**4. What is the map NOT showing?**
- What components are conspicuously absent?
- What would a competitor's map look like?
- What does the map assume is stable that might not be?

**5. What does the map imply?**
- What decisions should change based on this map?
- What patterns are visible that weren't visible before?
- What should we start/stop/continue based on this?

## Building Multiple Maps

For complex organizations, a single map is rarely sufficient.

### Map Types

**Current State Map**: The landscape as it is today.

**Future State Map**: The landscape as it will be in 3–5 years (based on climatic patterns).

**Desired State Map**: The landscape as you want to be positioned in 3–5 years.

**Competitor Map**: The landscape from a key competitor's perspective.

**Ecosystem Map**: The landscape including suppliers, partners, and ecosystem members.

### Working with Map Sets
- Compare current state and future state to identify transitions you must manage
- Compare current state and desired state to identify gaps
- Compare your map and competitor maps to identify asymmetries

## The Living Map

Maps should be living artifacts, not documents:

### Update Triggers
Update your map when:
- A new competitor enters the market
- A key component shows evidence of evolutionary movement
- A major technology shift is announced
- A significant business decision is made
- A previously "stable" assumption turns out to be wrong

### Map Versioning
Keep historical versions of maps. Comparing a current map with a map from 2 years ago shows:
- Which components evolved as expected
- Which were surprises
- Whether your anticipation was accurate

This retrospective improves future anticipation.

### Map Reviews
Schedule regular map reviews:
- **Monthly**: Review fast-moving components (technology, competitor activity)
- **Quarterly**: Full map review with leadership team
- **Annual**: Strategic review — are the major positioning decisions still sound?

## Common Advanced Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Map Hoarding
Creating maps and keeping them secret destroys their value. Maps must be shared and challenged to be useful.

**Fix**: Default to sharing; restrict only when genuinely necessary.

### Mistake 2: Over-Precision
Placing components at very specific positions (e.g., "this component is at exactly 0.73 on the evolution axis").

**Fix**: Think in the four stages with fuzzy boundaries. The value is relative position, not absolute.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring the Emotional Dimension
Maps often reveal uncomfortable truths. Presenting map findings without managing the emotional response destroys adoption.

**Fix**: Create psychological safety before the map review. Frame findings as "what the landscape shows" rather than "what you're doing wrong."

### Mistake 4: Confusing the Map with Reality
The map is a model. Reality is more complex. Using the map to suppress important nuance is a mistake.

**Fix**: Treat maps as hypotheses to be refined, not truths to be defended.

### Mistake 5: Solving on the Map
Using the map review session to also develop the strategy response.

**Fix**: Separate map reviews from strategy sessions. Build shared understanding of the landscape first; then develop strategy.

## Developing Mapping Intuition

### The Newspaper Test for Evolution
How would a journalist describe this component? If they would use commodity language ("just another utility"), it's a commodity. If they'd describe it as novel and interesting, it might be genesis or custom.

### The "When Was This Invented?" Heuristic
Very roughly:
- Invented in last 5 years: likely Genesis
- Invented 5–15 years ago: likely Custom→Product
- Invented 15–30 years ago: likely Product→Commodity
- Invented 30+ years ago: likely Commodity

This is very rough — some things commoditize in 3 years; some take 50. But it's a useful starting point.

### The Competitive Dynamics Test
Look at competitive behavior in the space:
- **No competition** (too early): Genesis
- **Specialist competition** (few players, premium pricing): Custom
- **Feature competition** (many players, marketing differentiation): Product
- **Price competition** (many players, no differentiation): Commodity

### The "Would You Build This?" Test
If a new startup were to enter your market, would they build this component or buy/rent it?
- If they'd build it: it's still differentiated (Custom or early Product)
- If they'd definitely buy or rent it: it's a Product or Commodity

## Teaching Wardley Mapping

### The Core Curriculum
For teaching mapping to a team:
1. The Sun Tzu problem (why we need maps)
2. The two axes (value chain + evolution)
3. Building a simple map (tea shop exercise)
4. The four stages (characteristics of each)
5. Climatic patterns (what always happens)
6. Doctrine (what always works)
7. Gameplay (what depends on context)

### Common Learner Mistakes
- Confusing value chain position (Y) with importance: emphasize it's about visibility
- Placing things based on adoption, not evolution: use the characteristics grid
- Building maps that show what they want to believe: create challenge processes

### Sustained Adoption
Mapping dies without sustained practice. To embed mapping in an organization:
- Use maps for real decisions (not just exercises)
- Create a champion in each team
- Build mapping into strategic planning cycles
- Celebrate when maps led to good decisions

# Doctrine — Universal Principles for Strategic Success

Reference for Wardley's doctrine: the universal good practices that apply regardless of context.

## What is Doctrine?

Doctrine is a set of **universal principles** — practices that are beneficial regardless of where you are in the landscape. They are not context-specific (that's gameplay). They are the foundational behaviors that make an organization capable of strategic thinking and execution.

Wardley identifies ~40 doctrinal principles organized into phases.

> "Doctrine is what you do before you know what you need to do."

## Why Doctrine Matters

Without doctrinal fitness, you cannot execute strategy effectively. A strategically brilliant plan executed by an organization with poor doctrine will fail. Most organizations fail not from bad strategy but from poor doctrine.

Think of doctrine as **organizational fitness** — the baseline capability required to do anything well.

## Phase 1: Stop Self-Harm (Basic Hygiene)

These are the most fundamental principles. Violating them actively destroys value.

### 1. Know Your Users
- Understand who your users are, what they need, and why
- Segment users appropriately — different users need different things
- Build empathy for user needs, not just user desires

### 2. Use a Common Language
- Develop shared vocabulary for discussing the landscape
- Without common language, communication is approximate and misleading
- Maps are a form of common language

### 3. Be Transparent
- Share maps, plans, and reasoning openly within the organization
- Transparency enables collaboration and challenges bad assumptions
- Secrecy breeds silos and bad decisions

### 4. Focus on User Needs
- Everything should trace back to a user need
- Activities that don't serve user needs are waste
- Regularly audit initiatives against actual user value

### 5. Remove Duplication and Bias
- Duplication: building the same component in multiple teams is waste
- Bias: decisions based on who argues loudest, not what maps show

### 6. Use Appropriate Methods
- Different evolutionary stages require different management approaches
- Do not apply agile to commodity components; do not apply six sigma to genesis
- More on this in Chapter 17 (Infinity and Beyond)

## Phase 2: Becoming More Context-Aware

### 7. Think Small (Cells/Teams)
- Organize around small, autonomous teams
- Each team should own a specific part of the value chain
- Two-pizza teams (small enough to be fed by two pizzas) are not just Amazon dogma — they reflect an understanding of communication overhead

### 8. Understand Your Landscape
- Map your environment before deciding on actions
- The map comes before the strategy
- Revisit maps regularly as landscapes change

### 9. Use Appropriate Management
- Genesis: manage with autonomy and exploration
- Custom: manage with skill and project discipline
- Product: manage with product practices
- Commodity: manage with process and efficiency

### 10. Move Fast
- The landscape is always moving; standing still is moving backward
- Speed matters, but speed in the wrong direction is still wrong
- Maps enable fast movement in the right direction

### 11. Be Pragmatic
- Good enough today beats perfect tomorrow
- Avoid perfectionism in fast-moving environments
- Fail fast, learn fast, iterate

### 12. Think Aptitude and Attitude
- Match people's aptitudes and attitudes to evolutionary stages
- Pioneers are wrong for commodity operations
- Town planners stifle genesis work

## Phase 3: Better for Less

### 13. Optimize Flow
- Remove bottlenecks in the value chain
- Look for components that are blocking evolution
- Lean and flow thinking applies here

### 14. Think Small (Contracts)
- Small, short contracts over large, long ones
- Enables faster iteration and course correction
- Reduces lock-in and inertia

### 15. Distribute Power and Decision-Making
- Move decisions as close to the information as possible
- Centralized command is slow and error-prone
- Enable teams to act without constant approval

### 16. Seek the Best
- Hire well; the best people in the right roles matter enormously
- Competence compounds — great teams attract great people

### 17. Commit to Change
- Strategy is worthless without commitment to execute
- Avoid half-measures
- Create momentum

## Phase 4: Listening

### 18. Be Humble
- You are often wrong about the landscape
- Seek challenges to your assumptions
- Maps that have never been challenged are probably wrong

### 19. Take Small Steps
- Especially in uncertain territory (genesis)
- Each step reveals information for the next step
- Avoid large, irreversible commitments in uncertain landscapes

### 20. Use Evidence
- Base decisions on observable data, not gut feel
- Challenge assertions that cannot be grounded in evidence
- Create feedback loops

### 21. Bias Toward Action
- Analysis paralysis is real and costly
- A good decision today beats a perfect decision in six months
- Especially in fast-moving landscapes

## Phase 5: Optimizing for Learning

### 22. A Bias Toward Open
- Open source, open standards, and transparency accelerate learning
- Being open is asymmetrically beneficial — others build on your work AND you learn from theirs
- Open source commoditizes components faster (useful if you don't want to own that space)

### 23. Think Fast, Inexpensive, Restrained, and Elegant (FIRE)
A replacement for traditional MVP/lean approaches with more emphasis on small, targeted actions:
- **Fast**: Deploy quickly
- **Inexpensive**: Minimize capital at risk
- **Restrained**: Don't overbuild
- **Elegant**: Simple solutions are more reliable

### 24. Manage Inertia
- Identify and explicitly manage sources of organizational inertia
- Past success creates inertia — this is the most dangerous kind
- More in Chapter 8 (Keeping Wolves at Bay)

### 25. Setting Exceptional Standards
- Excellence should be the expectation, not the exception
- Poor practices become entrenched; start with high standards

## Doctrine vs. Gameplay

| | Doctrine | Gameplay |
|--|----------|---------|
| **Context** | Universal | Landscape-specific |
| **When** | Always | When the situation calls for it |
| **Example** | Be transparent | Attack with open source |
| **Failure mode** | Violating doctrine always hurts | Wrong gameplay can still work with good doctrine |

## Common Doctrinal Failures

### 1. Applying the Wrong Method
Using agile practices for commodity components (e.g., agile-ing your way through managing electricity procurement). Result: waste of specialist attention on non-differentiating work.

### 2. Centralized Decision-Making
Requiring senior approval for operational decisions. Result: slowness, context loss, and demoralization.

### 3. Building Commodities
Spending engineering effort on components that are available as utilities. Result: expensive, high-maintenance, non-differentiating infrastructure.

### 4. No Common Language
Different teams using different words for the same things, or the same words for different things. Result: misalignment, duplicated effort, poor communication.

### 5. Ignoring Users
Building what the organization thinks is valuable rather than what users need. Result: products no one uses.

## The Doctrine Quadrants

Organizations can be assessed across two dimensions:
- **Doctrinal fitness**: How well do they follow universal good practices?
- **Situational awareness**: How well do they understand their landscape?

| | Low Awareness | High Awareness |
|--|--------------|---------------|
| **Good Doctrine** | Well-organized but navigating blind | Excellent — can execute strategy |
| **Poor Doctrine** | Random walk — luck-dependent | Can see the landscape but can't act on it |

Most organizations are in the bottom-left quadrant. The goal is the top-right.

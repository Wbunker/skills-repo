# On Being Lost — Situational Awareness

Reference for why maps matter, the problem of strategic navigation without maps, and the foundations of Wardley Mapping.

## The Core Problem: No Maps in Business

### The Landscape Analogy
Most businesses operate without maps. Imagine an army general who:
- Has no map of the battlefield
- Cannot see where troops are positioned
- Doesn't know the terrain
- Must rely on gut instinct and past experience alone

This is the reality of most strategic planning. We use tools like SWOT, BCG matrices, and competitor analysis — but none of these are maps.

### What Makes Something a Map
A true map has:
1. **Visual representation** of the space
2. **Components** — the things that exist in the space
3. **Position** — where things are relative to each other
4. **Movement** — how things change over time
5. **Context** — the environment in which decisions are made

SWOT analysis has none of these properties. It is a list, not a map.

## The Five Stages of Situational Awareness

Awareness correlates strongly with competitive success:

| Stage | Description | Strategic Capability |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| **1 — Unaware** | No knowledge of environment | Reactive only |
| **2 — Aware** | Some knowledge, no structure | Basic pattern matching |
| **3 — Operational** | Understands components and position | Tactical planning |
| **4 — Strategic** | Understands movement and change | Anticipation possible |
| **5 — Mastery** | Full landscape awareness | Shaping the environment |

Most organizations operate at Stage 1–2. Wardley Mapping aims for Stage 4–5.

## Why Existing Strategy Tools Fail

### SWOT Analysis
- Subjective — different people produce different SWOTs for the same company
- No positional context — "strong brand" is meaningless without knowing the landscape
- No movement — static snapshot that ignores evolution
- No causality — can't reason about why things are the way they are

### Porter's Five Forces
- Useful but positional — tells you about the current landscape, not how it will change
- No evolutionary dimension
- Can't distinguish between a commodity and a genesis-stage component

### BCG Matrix (Stars/Cash Cows/Dogs/Question Marks)
- Position is based on market share and growth, not evolutionary stage
- Does not help with build vs. buy vs. outsource decisions
- Cannot anticipate disruption

### The Common Failure Mode
All these tools produce artifacts that **look** like strategy but are actually lists of opinions dressed up in frameworks. They give the impression of rigor without the substance of it.

## Sun Tzu and the Art of Strategy

Simon Wardley draws heavily from *The Art of War*:

> "If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles."

Sun Tzu's principles applied to business:
- **Know the terrain** → understand the landscape (the map)
- **Know yourself** → honest assessment of capabilities
- **Know the enemy** → understand competitors' positions
- **Timing** → act at the right evolutionary moment

The five factors Sun Tzu says determine victory:
1. **Moral law** — Purpose and culture
2. **Heaven** — Timing (climatic patterns)
3. **Earth** — Landscape (the map)
4. **Commander** — Leadership and doctrine
5. **Method and discipline** — Execution

Wardley Mapping addresses "Earth" — the landscape — which Sun Tzu says you must understand before all else.

## The Meaning of Strategy

### Strategy is NOT
- A vision statement
- A set of goals
- A plan
- A budget
- A list of initiatives

### Strategy IS
A coherent approach to navigating an uncertain landscape, using:
- Understanding of where you are (position on the map)
- Understanding of where things are going (evolution)
- Understanding of available moves (gameplay)
- Clear purpose (why you are doing this)

### The Cycle of Strategy
```
Observe → Orient → Decide → Act → (repeat)
     ↑___________________________________|
```
The OODA Loop (Boyd): maps enable better "Orient" — the hardest and most important step.

## The Problem of Copying Without Context

A common failure: copying what successful companies do without understanding *why* those practices work.

### The Crossing the River Example
Company A uses agile development and succeeds. Company B sees this and copies agile. But Company B is in a different landscape — they have commodity components where Company A had genesis-stage ones. Agile is not appropriate everywhere.

**Without a map, you cannot know:**
- Whether a practice is appropriate for your context
- Whether your situation resembles the company you're copying
- What the second-order effects of the practice will be

### Copying Amazon's "Two-Pizza Teams"
Amazon's structure emerged from their specific landscape. Copying the structure without understanding the map behind it will not produce the same results.

## The Bias Problem

Humans are systematically bad at strategy without maps:

### Common Cognitive Biases in Strategy
| Bias | How it manifests |
|------|-----------------|
| **Confirmation bias** | Seeking data that supports existing plans |
| **Survivorship bias** | Learning from success stories, ignoring failures |
| **Recency bias** | Overweighting recent events and trends |
| **Dunning-Kruger** | Overconfidence in areas of low competence |
| **Sunk cost fallacy** | Continuing failed initiatives because of past investment |
| **Status quo bias** | Preference for the current state of affairs |

Maps provide a shared, external artifact that partially counteracts these biases by making assumptions explicit and visible.

## Key Insight: The Landscape is Not Optional

You are always operating in a landscape. The question is whether you are navigating it consciously with a map, or stumbling through it blind.

Companies that succeed consistently are not luckier — they have better situational awareness. They know where they are, where they're going, and what moves are available to them.

> "Without a map, all strategies are equally valid. With a map, most strategies are obviously wrong."
> — Simon Wardley
